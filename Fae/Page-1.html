The Windswept Garden
I was born on a boat in a fleet of many boats. I was told that it stormed that night. We were a nomadic people, with no homes other than our rafts and our ships, and the islands we visited to fill our stocks by trading our caught fish and foraging for our ropes and wood and sails. My mother was the matriarch, and she was a woman of deep magical faith. Her rituals commanded the winds and quelled the storms. My father was her god. I know now that he was only a man. A powerful man, but only a man. But they worshiped him nonetheless. They worshiped him for his might, his roiling clouds and raging thunder. He struck fear in their hearts and they wished to be like him, to be ruled by him. And he wished to rule them.



My birth was a blessing, and I was raised both as god and as mortal. My father wished to have me in his pantheon, his firstborn, his prize, his prodigal son and power to command. My mother wished to have me on her boat, to brush my hair and teach me how to fish, to sail, to swim, to trade. Her beloved, her beautiful daughter.



I learned from them both. One night I would play games with the other children, the next I would rock the ships and toss the waves to force the tributes of mortals. We played on a large and flat raft, we played a game with sticks and hoops, a game with dice and fake coins, a game with closed eyes and hushed whispers. At night, my mother would hold me in her lap and teach me the names of the stars.



My father grew angry. I had not inherited his thunder, and that greatly wounded his pride. Though he would never admit it, I knew his anger was mixed with satisfaction that I would never be able to challenge him in his might.



Our people would eat, and I would watch, as a god like myself does not eat. I had to learn how to breathe, breathe in a divine way. In a way that sustained me, turned my flesh to water and stitched me together with flows of air. My friends did not like the way I played. I floated above them to avoid losing, I swam out from solid wood to startle them. They loathed me, for I was nothing like them. And they loved me, for I cleared the skies and pointed the winds for our sails to catch. I stilled the water, and I brought great bounty from the ocean.



In the eyes of our children, there was nothing more important than dance. It was everything, it was our pride, our stories, our ancestors come to see us in our prosperity and our glory. Great drums and flutes and horns and strings, passing sailors could hear our voices ring for miles around, and they knew who we were, though they would never see our faces. Such was the power of our music. In the eyes of our elders, there was nothing more important than the song.



I conquered it. I spun, and my passage was known by the dampness in the air and the ringing of bells. We were naked if not for the light painted onto us, written in great symbols and stories we gave to each other alight in gemstone hues. It was the only time I ever saw my mother cry. The music never made me cry. I didn’t feel the pride of our ancestors. I felt no comfort, no glory, no euphoria. I only felt watched.



My fathers anger reached new bounds. His training turned cruel and pointed. He struck me down with a hammer of thunder, and I was defenseless against it. But I felt something new. I felt power. The lightning, his lightning, flowed like neon in the glass of my body, and I failed to suppress it. It arced out, licking everything, scorching our tapestries, decimating our people, searing my skin. I awoke in a shallow grave. Believing me dead, my father had buried me next to my mother.



A merchant ship picked me up, having seen the destruction from far away. Their kindness was cut short by a pirate raid. I cared little for either of them. As they were taking their captives to sell and to ransom, I heard the captain discuss selling my “pretty eyes”. For that, I saw how well he could swim without his hands or his feet.



I declared myself the god of these pirates, and I led them to glory and fortune. We menaced the sea; any ships that challenged us were swallowed by the waters and hounded by endless storms.



I dreamed of fire and gold, and it blinded my eyes. I was vengeful, my people lost and their culture lost to me. I would never know why the music made my mother cry. I was still stuck in that shallow grave, buried next to her. It was my ghost that haunted that ship.



It would make me lose the crew as well. One of them, a young man, I liked. He had fled his family, fearing military service, to make it rich as an outlaw. He was cunning and cruel, and I admired him for it. His head was mounted on a pike by the same military he had escaped. They were powerful, and they came prepared to free the routes of their trade ships from our terror.



It was the second time I felt my father’s lightning. It bubbled up, deep and unseen until it made itself violently known. It might have given us the victory, had it not been equally devastating to everyone involved, burning its mark further into my skin.



I washed up on the shore snagged on driftwood and suffocated by weeds. I had survived. Again.



I wandered for some time, floating between towns and villages nestled in rolling and foggy hills. I meditated, for I did not sleep. I did not sleep, for I dreamed. I dreamed of fire and gold and bones settled in the sands of the sea floor. I dreamed of ghosts and blood and music. I dreamed of nerves set ablaze with flickering sparks.



The people asked me who I was, what I was. I had no words to answer them, but they filled that blank themselves. They called a bard, and I played the part. My music was both my greeting and my exit.



Many would try to court me, more would fantasize of doing so. Dull boys would offer me flowers and expect something in return. Nervous girls would sew me cloaks I could not wear; they would fall right through me. Older men would look at me and dream of a youthful life they had never gotten to live. I did not care for any of them.



Except, perhaps, one man. Nobody ever saw him during the day, though at night he would prove skilled with the fiddle. He was pale, gangly, and ugly. His face resembled that of a rat. I enjoyed playing along with his music.



One night, something was wrong with him. He was clammy, delirious, strange. I followed him out into the night, where he devoured one of the local farmers. I saw the fear and panic in him when I made myself known, I could see the plans racing by in his eyes. He would have to kill me, flee town, make a new life for himself. He flashed his fangs and lunged for me, though he was easy to restrain.



I didn’t care much at all.I gave him an alibi, told the people that I had seen him that night, scribbling away at his papers like he always was. At the farmer’s funeral, he made a rather morbid joke accompanied by his toothy smile. It was our little secret, and he removed his mask around me when he could.



I liked him. Though I left all the same.



In my travels, I heard rumors of a magic that could rouse the souls of the dead, bring them enough into the space near you to speak to and touch. It was a power I coveted, and hunted with no reprieve.



It led me to an old man. A man perhaps older than any man should be. He told me of the magic. It was real, and it was a song. There was no one left but him that could recall it.



He denied me the song. He would not teach it to me.



I stalked him for weeks on end, attacking him when he was vulnerable. He himself refused to confront me, though his child was happy to fight in his stead. Their name was Sinclair, and their wrath was like a flame from the heavens. I was stuck down again and again, returning each day to fight and be defeated.



Ragged, spent, lit by the glow of a small campfire, I declared to that wizard that I would haunt him for the rest of his days. Slowly, dryly, he told me that I had better make myself comfortable then. I obliged, but not out of choice. Sinclair tells me that I promptly collapsed.



I awoke undisturbed. I kept following them, but there was no further violence. As the seasons changed, so too did our contempt turn to acceptance, and we grew close.



Sinclair convinced me to aid them in repairing a roof. It had been destroyed by a storm the previous day, and Sinclair insisted that it was my fault for not halting it. If I had not worked to prevent this destruction, then I should work to fix it, they said. I had no energy to argue, so the roof was repaired by a morning’s worth of work. At the end of it, Sinclair called me their sister.



My now master taught me many things about wind and water and lightning. Sinclair was taught many things of fire. The prospect of the magic song went from a complete taboo topic to a potential coming of age gift.



We picked up another sister of mine, Kasumi, at the shore of a lake filled with blood. She looked at us with hatred, and the Master started back with horror. She was the only one the Master wanted to keep at a first glance. Though he made it seem like he hated it, his habit of collecting young lost children always got the better of him. Kasumi, with eyes like dark water and flesh dotted from thrashing bites. She meditated, for she did not sleep.



Last was Duke, who was picked up after we ventured out of Elenia and into Terathi. Duke was a young street thug, and I hated him for it. That was, until I saw the shining brilliance his soul could produce. We battled, and it called from me a controlled use of lightning. It surged within me, empowering me. Only then did I consider him a sister. His eyes stained like glass reflecting green in many facets, his body featureless and dull like a doll. He did not meditate, for he slept. And when he slept, he dreamed.



Though each of us, save Sinclair, have gone off to wander on our own, we keep eyes on each other.
